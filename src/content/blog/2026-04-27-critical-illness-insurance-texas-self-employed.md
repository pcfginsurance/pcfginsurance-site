# Critical Illness Insurance in Texas for Freelancers and the Self-Employed

Texas has over 1.7 million self-employed workers, one of the highest counts in the country. Independent contractors in Houston. Tech and creative freelancers in Austin. Consultants and service professionals in Dallas and San Antonio. Owner-operators across the rest of the state.

No state income tax gives self-employed Texans a real financial advantage. What it doesn't do is protect you when a serious medical diagnosis takes you out of work for six to twelve months and the income stops entirely.

This article covers what critical illness insurance is, what it realistically costs for Texans, and how to figure out whether it fills a gap in your current financial setup.

## What critical illness insurance is

Critical illness insurance pays you a tax-free lump sum when you're diagnosed with a covered condition. Most policies cover cancer, heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, major organ transplant, and several other serious diagnoses. The benefit is paid directly to you, not to a provider or facility. You decide how to use it.

Mortgage. Lost business income. Your plan's out-of-pocket maximum. Travel to MD Anderson or another specialty center for treatment. Childcare during a long recovery. The money is yours to allocate however your situation requires.

It's a supplemental product. It works alongside major medical coverage, not in place of it. Your health plan handles medical bills. Critical illness insurance handles the financial disruption your health plan doesn't cover.

## Why self-employed Texans face real exposure here

**Texas has no state short-term disability program.** New York, California, and a handful of other states have mandatory state-run disability programs that provide some income replacement when you can't work. Texas does not. Self-employed Texans with no employer-sponsored disability coverage have zero automatic income protection if a serious diagnosis takes them out of work.

**Austin, Houston, and Dallas have gotten expensive.** The cost-of-living narrative about Texas has shifted over the last several years. Austin in particular has seen significant increases in housing and living costs. A freelancer in Austin or Houston running a lean operation can burn through six months of savings faster than the income gap closes.

**The cancer exposure is above average.** Texas has a higher incidence of several cancer types compared to national averages, according to the Texas Cancer Registry. The state is also home to MD Anderson Cancer Center in Houston, one of the top cancer treatment facilities in the world. But top-tier specialty care often comes with significant out-of-network costs, even on strong commercial plans. That difference lands on you.

## What a serious diagnosis actually costs in Texas

The American Journal of Public Health reports that roughly 66% of US personal bankruptcies are tied to medical events. The Kaiser Family Foundation reports 41% of US adults currently carry medical debt.

For a self-employed Texan, a realistic picture of exposure during a serious diagnosis year includes: major medical out-of-pocket costs of $8,000 to $18,000, six to twelve months of reduced or lost business income, out-of-network specialist costs, travel and lodging if you're receiving treatment away from home, and fixed living expenses that don't stop because your income did.

Your health plan covers the medical side. Nothing covers the rest of it unless you've planned for it.

## What coverage looks like

Lump sum benefits typically range from $10,000 to $500,000. For most healthy adults under 70, simplified underwriting policies up to $75,000 are available with no medical exam. You answer health questions on the application and typically get a decision within a few days.

Monthly premiums depend on age, health, tobacco use, and benefit amount. A healthy 40-year-old non-smoker typically sees premiums in the $25 to $60 range per month. The right benefit amount depends on your fixed monthly costs, your current savings runway, and how long you'd realistically need to stabilize your finances through a recovery period.

## Three numbers worth knowing

Your annual out-of-pocket maximum on your current health plan. That's your direct medical cost floor if a serious year hits.

Your monthly fixed expenses multiplied by 12. That's what it costs to keep your life running for a full year with no business income coming in.

Your liquid savings today. That's the gap buffer you actually have.

Most self-employed Texans find the gap between their savings and their total exposure is meaningful. Critical illness insurance is a cost-efficient way to bridge that gap before you need it.
